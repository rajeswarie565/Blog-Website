My First Step into Web Development

Published on 1 September 2025 · Tags: HTML, CSS, JavaScript

Every journey starts with a single step, and this is mine. Building this blog with plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript is my way of understanding how the web actually works under the hood.

Instead of relying on heavy frameworks, I wanted to write everything by hand: the structure, the design, and the interactivity. It’s slower at first, but it teaches me how each part of a website fits together.

In this post, I’m reminding myself that it’s completely fine to start small. A simple page with a clean layout and a clear purpose is already a win. The important thing is to keep building, keep breaking things, and keep learning.

Finding Creativity in Code

Published on 5 October 2025 · Tags: Design, JavaScript

Many people think coding is only about logic, rules, and strict syntax. But the more I code, the more I realize how creative it can be. Choosing colors, typography, and layouts is just as important as making sure the JavaScript runs without errors.

Even something as simple as a comment form can be “designed.” How does it feel to use? Is the spacing comfortable? Does the button invite a click? These little decisions turn a plain page into a friendly experience.

For me, the most satisfying moment is when structure, design, and interactivity all come together. That’s when a collection of files starts to feel like a real website.

How I Stay Consistent While Learning

Published on 10 November 2025 · Tags: Learning, Productivity

Learning web development can feel overwhelming. There are new tools, new concepts, and constant updates. What helps me most is not speed, but consistency. Even 30 focused minutes a day can move me forward.

I like to split my time into three parts: reading or watching, practicing, and then reflecting. This blog is actually part of that reflection step. Writing about what I learned forces me to understand it clearly.

By building small projects like this blog, I’m training myself to finish what I start. That habit is more valuable than memorizing every single method or keyword.
